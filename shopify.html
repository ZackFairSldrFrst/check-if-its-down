Shopify is the world's leading e-commerce platform, powering millions of online stores across 175 countries. From small businesses and independent entrepreneurs to major brands and enterprises, Shopify provides the infrastructure for merchants to sell products online, in person, and everywhere in between.

What Causes Shopify Outages?

Third-Party Infrastructure Dependencies

Like many major platforms, Shopify relies on third-party services for content delivery and security. The November 18, 2025 outage was caused by problems with Cloudflare, Shopify's CDN provider. When these infrastructure partners experience issues, Shopify stores can become inaccessible even when Shopify's own systems are functioning properly.

High Traffic Events

Major shopping events can sometimes strain Shopify's infrastructure:

  • Black Friday and Cyber Monday sales
  • Flash sales and limited product drops
  • Viral products that suddenly drive massive traffic
  • Holiday shopping seasons

Platform Updates and Maintenance

Shopify regularly updates its platform with new features and improvements. While these updates are typically seamless, they can occasionally cause temporary disruptions, especially if they involve core infrastructure changes.

Database and Backend Issues

Problems with Shopify's database systems, payment processing infrastructure, or API services can affect store functionality, checkout processes, or admin panel access.

Impact on Merchants

Direct Revenue Loss

When Shopify goes down, merchants can't process sales. For the November 18 outage that occurred during peak morning shopping hours, many merchants experienced:

  • Complete store inaccessibility for customers
  • Inability to process orders or payments
  • Lost sales opportunities during the 3.5-hour downtime
  • Abandoned carts and frustrated customers

Operational Disruptions

Beyond lost sales, outages affect daily operations:

  • Cannot access admin panel to manage inventory
  • Unable to fulfill or ship existing orders
  • Can't respond to customer service inquiries
  • Marketing campaigns may continue driving traffic to inaccessible stores

Small Business Impact

Outages are particularly challenging for small businesses that:

  • Rely entirely on Shopify for income
  • Operate on thin profit margins
  • Can't afford extended periods of downtime
  • May not have backup sales channels

How to Check If Shopify Is Down

1. Use This Status Page: Our free Shopify down detector monitors the platform in real-time and provides immediate updates on detected issues.

2. Test Different Components:

  • Try accessing your storefront from different devices/networks
  • Check if the admin panel (myshopify.com) is accessible
  • Test checkout functionality if the store loads
  • Verify the Shopify mobile app is working

3. Official Status Page: Visit status.shopify.com for updates directly from Shopify (note: this page may also be affected during CDN outages).

4. Check Error Messages:

  • "Store unavailable" - Likely a platform-wide or CDN issue
  • "503 Service Unavailable" - Server-side problems
  • Infinite loading - Possible CDN or routing issues
  • Checkout errors - May indicate payment processing issues

What to Do During a Shopify Outage

For Merchants:

  • Pause Paid Advertising: Stop ads driving traffic to your inaccessible store
  • Communicate with Customers: Use social media to inform customers about the outage
  • Monitor This Page: Check for real-time updates on resolution status
  • Document Impact: Track lost sales for potential compensation claims
  • Prepare for Recovery: Be ready for surge in orders once service is restored

For Shoppers:

  • Be patient - the issue is typically temporary
  • Save items you want to purchase and try again later
  • Check back within a few hours - most outages resolve quickly
  • Contact merchants through social media if urgent

Preventing Impact from Future Outages

Diversify Sales Channels: Don't rely solely on Shopify. Consider:

  • Selling on marketplaces (Amazon, eBay, Etsy)
  • Maintaining social media selling options
  • Having a backup payment processor
  • Building an email list to communicate during outages

Business Continuity Planning:

  • Have a communication plan for outages
  • Consider business interruption insurance
  • Document all outages for records
  • Build customer loyalty to retain business after disruptions
